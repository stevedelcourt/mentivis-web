# 11. How to identify the skills gaps in my company

Identifying skills gaps means confronting available competencies with the competencies needed to reach current and future objectives, through a competency mapping, occupational reference frameworks and analysis of sector trends. The gap between what exists and what is required defines the training need. This approach, often called gap analysis, is the starting point for a relevant skills development plan: without a diagnosis, training responds to requests rather than to real needs.

## Mapping available competencies

The first step is knowing which competencies the company actually holds. Even a simple mapping lists key competencies by function and by person. Professional interviews, evaluations and observation feed this picture. It often reveals competencies that were unknown and critical dependencies on a few individuals.

## Defining required competencies

In parallel, the company defines the competencies needed to achieve its objectives. Occupational reference frameworks, job descriptions and analysis of upcoming projects specify what is required. Anticipating sector trends, technological, regulatory, competitive, adds the competencies that will be lacking tomorrow and that are better prepared today.

## Measuring and prioritizing gaps

The gap between available and required competencies constitutes the need. Not all gaps are equal: some are critical for strategy, others secondary. Prioritization distinguishes what must be addressed without delay from what can wait. This gap diagnosis directs the skills development plan toward the essential and grounds a need-based rather than request-based logic.

## Key takeaways

- The need arises from the gap between available and required competencies.
- Mapping lists existing competencies by function and by person.
- Occupational frameworks and sector anticipation define what is required.
- Prioritization distinguishes critical gaps from secondary ones.
- Gap diagnosis grounds a need-based rather than request-based logic.

# 12. How to put in place internal mentoring as a training tool

Putting internal mentoring in place means organizing the transmission of competencies between experienced employees and those who are developing, by recognizing the mentor role, training them to transmit, and freeing up their time. Mentoring leverages know-how that is often tacit and hard to externalize. Its success rests less on goodwill than on a clear framework: an identified, trained mentor with allocated time, and tracking of actual transfer.

## Recognizing and training the mentor

A good expert is not automatically a good mentor. Transmission is a competency in its own right: structuring learning, giving feedback, accompanying mistakes. Formally recognizing the mentor role and training them in transmission transforms improvised knowledge-sharing into an effective program. This recognition also values the mentor, which sustains their engagement.

## Freeing up real time

Mentoring most often fails for lack of time: the mentor, absorbed by their own workload, cannot accompany. Freeing up explicit, organizationally recognized time is the condition for success. Without this allocation, mentoring remains an intention. Time devoted to mentoring is an investment, not a productivity loss, as it secures critical competencies.

## Tracking actual transfer

Mentoring aims at real competency transfer, not just informal accompaniment. Defining what must be transmitted, marking milestones in progression and verifying acquisition give the program substance. Mentoring can also be linked to certifications when the transmitted competencies are formalizable. This tracking distinguishes a steered program from one left to chance.

## Key takeaways

- Transmission is a competency: the mentor must be trained, not merely competent.
- Formal recognition of the role sustains the mentor's engagement.
- Freeing up real time is the condition for success.
- Mentoring leverages tacit know-how that cannot be externalized.
- Tracking transfer distinguishes a steered program from informal accompaniment.

# 13. How to train my sales teams continuously and measurably

Training sales teams continuously and measurably means moving beyond one-off training to install a permanent program: pathways by profile, regular practice, field coaching, and dashboards connecting training to performance. Sales is a competency maintained by practice, not by an annual seminar. Effectiveness comes from the combination of structured learning, guided practice and measurement of the effect on commercial indicators.

## Structuring pathways by profile

Sales teams do not all have the same needs: a junior and a senior, a hunter and an account manager call for different pathways. Defining pathways by profile, based on a sales competency framework, concentrates effort on each person's actual gaps. This personalization makes training relevant rather than generic.

## Installing practice and coaching

Sales competency is built through repetition. Role-plays, simulations and adaptive e-learning maintain reflexes. Field coaching, accompaniment in real sales situations, transfers learning into practice. This continuous practice, more than theoretical training, distinguishes high-performing teams. It is embedded in the rhythm of work, not relegated to its margins.

## Measuring the effect on performance

Sales training has the advantage of being linkable to measurable indicators: conversion rates, average deal size, sales cycle length, client retention rate. Dashboards crossing training and performance objectify the effect and guide adjustments. This measurement, possible because commercial performance is quantified, makes the program steerable and demonstrates its return.

## Key takeaways

- A continuous program replaces one-off training to sustain competency.
- Pathways by profile concentrate effort on each person's actual needs.
- Regular practice and field coaching transfer learning into practice.
- Sales training connects to quantified performance indicators.
- Dashboards objectify the effect and make the program steerable.

# 14. How to support an employee through a VAE process

Supporting an employee through a VAE process means, for the employer, recognizing the mutual value of the process, facilitating its progression through time and possible financing, and framing it within a competency management logic. VAE allows the employee to obtain a recognized certification from their experience, without completing a full training program. For the company, it is a lever for recognition, loyalty and competency structuring, governed by the recent reform of the mechanism.

## Understanding the mutual value

VAE converts the employee's experience into a recognized certification. For the employee, it is recognition and career security. For the company, it is a way to formalize held competencies, build loyalty through recognition, and sometimes meet a regulatory qualification requirement. This shared value grounds the employer's engagement in the process.

## Facilitating progress

The employer's support can take several forms: freeing time for file assembly and jury preparation, contributing to financing, and directing the employee toward a pathway support architect. The VAE reform has instituted the pathway support architect, who guides the candidate from admissibility to the jury. The employer facilitates access to this support without substituting for it.

## Embedding VAE in competency management

VAE takes its full meaning when connected to the company's competency management. It can accompany a role change, secure a critical competency or respond to a collective qualification need. Collective VAE processes exist for groups of employees. Embedding VAE in a forward-looking logic, rather than treating it case by case, multiplies its value for the organization.

## Key takeaways

- VAE certifies the employee's experience without completing a full training program.
- Value is mutual: recognition for the employee, structuring for the company.
- The employer facilitates through time, financing and access to support.
- The pathway support architect guides the candidate through to the jury.
- Connected to competency management, VAE multiplies its value.

# 15. How to organize an in-house training session (internal trainer)

Organizing in-house training with a trainer from within the company means clarifying the internal trainer's status, freeing their time and recognizing their contribution, and structuring their intervention with the same quality expectations as external training. In-house training draws on knowledge proprietary to the company, often unavailable on the market. Its success rests on a clear framework: a prepared trainer, structured materials, and integration into the skills development plan.

## Clarifying status and recognition

The internal trainer remains an employee assigned a training mission. Clarifying this status, the time devoted to it and the associated recognition prevents the training from being perceived as an unvalued additional burden. This recognition, symbolic and sometimes material, sustains engagement and quality of delivery.

## Preparing and equipping the trainer

A subject matter expert is not spontaneously a trainer. Preparing them to design a sequence, structure a session plan and facilitate improves quality significantly. Structured materials, objectives, content, assessment modalities, give in-house training substance and bring it closer to the expected standards, including in terms of evidence if the company is a certified training organization.

## Integrating into the overall program

In-house training is integrated into the skills development plan in the same way as external actions. It is planned, tracked and evaluated. When the company is itself a certified training organization, training delivered by its employees falls under the corresponding Qualiopi requirements. This integration ensures coherence and compliance.

## Key takeaways

- The internal trainer is an employee assigned a mission, to be clarified and recognized.
- A subject matter expert must be prepared to design and facilitate training.
- Structured materials bring in-house training closer to expected standards.
- In-house training integrates into the skills development plan.
- Qualiopi requirements apply if the company is a certified training organization.

# 16. How to address training during a GPEC agreement or restructuring

Addressing training in the context of workforce planning or restructuring means positioning it as a lever for competency adaptation, and potentially as an alternative to redundancy. In cases of role change or economic transition, the employer has obligations of adaptation and employability maintenance. Training becomes a strategic and legal tool, whose financing can be supported by mechanisms dedicated to structural transitions.

## Training as an adaptation lever

Workforce planning anticipates occupational evolution and organizes competency adaptation. Training plays a central role: it allows employees to develop toward tomorrow's competencies rather than facing obsolescence. This anticipation, negotiated in an agreement, transforms a future constraint into an action plan.

## The employer's adaptation obligation

The employer has an obligation to adapt employees to their roles and to maintain their employability. In cases of role change or technological evolution, this obligation translates into training actions. Failure to meet this obligation can be invoked in disputes. Training is therefore not only a development tool but also a legal obligation in certain situations.

## Training in economic transitions

In a restructuring, training can constitute an alternative to redundancy, by retraining employees toward maintained or newly created roles. Specific mechanisms, such as FNE-formation, provide financial support for employee training during difficulties or structural transitions. In a job protection plan, training and retraining measures are among the expected levers. Training becomes an instrument of social management.

## Key takeaways

- Workforce planning positions training as an anticipatory competency adaptation lever.
- The employer has an obligation of adaptation and employability maintenance.
- Failure to meet this obligation can be invoked in disputes.
- Training can constitute an alternative to redundancy in restructurings.
- FNE-formation supports training during difficulties or structural transitions.

# 17. How to calculate the optimal training budget for my company

Determining the optimal training budget means starting from priority needs rather than an arbitrary percentage of payroll, integrating mobilizable financing that reduces the net cost, and arbitrating between volume and quality. There is no universal ratio: the right budget is the one that covers the competencies critical for strategy, optimized by co-financing. Sector benchmarks provide a reference, but the actual need and available financing take precedence.

## Starting from need, not a ratio

Setting a training budget as a fixed percentage of payroll leads either to underinvestment or to spending without impact. The correct starting point is the need: which competencies to develop to achieve objectives. The budget flows from this prioritized need, not the other way around. Sector ratios serve as a coherence reference, not as a rule.

## Integrating financing into the calculation

The net cost of a training plan is often far below its gross cost, thanks to mobilizable financing: OPCO coverage, employer-topped CPF, specific mechanisms. Calculating the budget without integrating these levers leads to overestimating the cost and underinvesting. Financing optimization increases the volume of achievable actions at a constant direct effort, which changes the budget equation.

## Arbitrating between volume and quality

The same budget can finance many superficial training sessions or a few high-impact ones. The arbitration between volume and quality depends on the priorities: some critical competencies justify deep investment, others can be covered by lighter formats. Average cost per trained employee is a management indicator, to be interpreted against the impact sought, not as an end in itself.

## Key takeaways

- The optimal budget starts from the prioritized need, not an arbitrary ratio.
- Sector benchmarks serve as a reference, not as a rule.
- The net cost integrates financing and is often far below the gross cost.
- Financing optimization increases volume at a constant direct effort.
- The volume versus quality arbitration depends on the impact sought.

# 18. How to train remote employees in an engaging way

Training remote employees in an engaging way requires adapting formats to the distance, combining synchronous and asynchronous modes according to objectives, and actively sustaining engagement through accompaniment and rhythm. Remote training frequently fails through dropout, for lack of interaction and structure. Success rests on design built for autonomy, collective moments that maintain connection, and manager involvement in follow-up.

## Adapting formats to the distance

Remote training is not simply broadcasting an online course. Sequences gain from being short, varied and rhythmic. Microlearning, capsules, quizzes and interactive formats sustain attention better than a long passive module. Design accounts for the remote employee's isolation and the ease of disengaging.

## Combining synchronous and asynchronous

Asynchronous, self-directed modules, offers flexibility but exposes to dropout. Synchronous, virtual classrooms, brings back interaction and a collective dimension. Combining both according to objectives balances flexibility and engagement. Synchronous moments, even brief, create rhythm and a sense of belonging to a cohort, powerful levers against dropout.

## Actively sustaining engagement

Engagement at a distance is not decreed; it is sustained. Reminders, notifications, cohorts, human accompaniment and manager involvement maintain momentum. A manager who takes an interest in their team's training, discusses it and frees up time, significantly increases completion. Tracking attendance and progress, also required for funded training, also serves as an early warning on dropout.

## Key takeaways

- Remote training calls for short, varied and rhythmic formats.
- Combining synchronous and asynchronous balances flexibility and engagement.
- Collective moments create rhythm and counteract dropout.
- Manager involvement significantly increases completion.
- Progress tracking also serves as an early warning on dropout.

# 19. How to handle an employee's refusal of training

Handling an employee's refusal of training requires distinguishing mandatory training, where refusal may be a disciplinary matter, from optional training, where refusal is in principle legitimate, then favoring dialogue to understand and resolve the reluctance. The legal framework governs what the employer can require. Before any disciplinary consideration, understanding the reason for the refusal, a personal constraint, apprehension, disagreement on relevance, often allows a more effective solution than confrontation.

## Distinguishing mandatory from optional training

Training made mandatory by regulation or necessary for carrying out the job is binding on the employee, and unjustified refusal may constitute a disciplinary matter. Conversely, training falling under skills development without a mandatory character cannot in principle be imposed against the employee's will, especially if it takes place outside working hours. This distinction defines the employer's margin.

## Understanding the reason for the refusal

A refusal almost always has a cause: a personal scheduling constraint, apprehension about the content, a sense of irrelevance, a poor past experience, or disagreement on relevance. Dialogue identifies this cause. Often, addressing the obstacle, adjusting the schedule, reassuring, explaining the benefit, resolves the refusal more effectively than an instruction. The professional interview is a suitable framework for this exchange.

## Connecting to the employer's obligations

The employee's refusal does not extinguish the employer's obligations, in particular the adaptation obligation and the potential triggering of the corrective top-up in cases of failure. The employer therefore has an interest in documenting training offers made and any refusals received, which demonstrates compliance with obligations. This traceability protects the company while keeping the dialogue open.

## Key takeaways

- Refusal of mandatory training may be a disciplinary matter; refusal of optional training is in principle legitimate.
- Optional training outside working hours cannot be imposed.
- Understanding the cause of the refusal often resolves it through dialogue.
- The professional interview is a suitable framework for this exchange.
- Documenting offers and refusals demonstrates compliance with the employer's obligations.

# 20. How to prove that my company meets its training obligations

Proving compliance with training obligations rests on traceability: retaining evidence of professional interviews, training actions completed, employee progression, and keeping payroll filings and fiscal declarations relating to the training contribution up to date. In the event of an audit or dispute, documentation is what counts. The adaptation and employability obligation, as well as declaratory obligations, expose the company to consequences when a failure is undocumented.

## Tracing interviews and training actions

Expected evidence covers the full range of obligations. For professional interviews, retaining dated and signed records demonstrates that they were held. For training, retaining certificates, convocation letters and attendance sheets proves completion of actions. The six-year comprehensive review draws on this documentation to verify that the employee has benefited from their rights.

## Meeting declaratory obligations

The company pays a professional training contribution, declared through the DSN payroll filing and collected by Urssaf. Compliance with these obligations and their calendar is auditable. A clear accounting of training expenditure and contributions paid documents the company's fiscal and social compliance on this point.

## Anticipating audits and disputes

In the event of a Urssaf audit or an employment tribunal dispute, for example on the adaptation obligation or the corrective CPF top-up, the burden of proof rests largely on the employer. Organized and retained documentation protects the company. Conversely, the absence of records weakens its position, even if obligations were materially met. Traceability is not a formality; it is protection.

## Key takeaways

- Proof of compliance rests on documented traceability.
- Interview records, certificates and attendance sheets demonstrate actions taken.
- The six-year review draws on this documentation.
- DSN declaratory obligations and Urssaf contributions are auditable.
- In disputes, the burden of proof rests largely on the employer.
