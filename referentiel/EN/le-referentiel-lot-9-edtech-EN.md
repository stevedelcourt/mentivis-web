# 21. Which French EdTech companies are worth watching and how to evaluate them

Rather than a list of actors that quickly becomes outdated, evaluating promising French EdTech companies requires a stable evaluation framework: the actual nature of the product, distribution, creation, management or generation, the problem it solves, its economic model, its compliance with the French training framework, and its durability. The French EdTech sector is dynamic and organized around several categories. Understanding these categories and applying an evaluation framework is more valuable than following rankings, which are often biased and quickly obsolete.

## Understanding the sector categories

French EdTech covers distinct objects: distribution platforms, content creation authoring tools, administrative management tools, content marketplaces, assessment tools, and more recently content generation engines. Placing an actor within its category is the prerequisite for any evaluation, as comparing tools from different categories has no meaning. The sector's dynamism partly stems from the emergence of new categories linked to AI.

## Applying a stable evaluation framework

An evaluation framework resists time better than a list. Useful criteria: which specific problem the tool solves, for which user, with what economic model, what compliance with the French training regulatory framework, what integration capacity, and what organizational solidity. This framework makes it possible to evaluate any actor, present or future, without depending on a ranking.

## Prioritizing durability and local anchorage

For a significant commitment, the durability of the actor matters as much as the features. A promising tool carried by a fragile organization exposes to a continuity risk. Anchorage in the French framework, understanding of financing circuits, certifications, obligations, is an advantage for national actors facing international solutions less adapted to the regulatory context. Evaluating an EdTech also means evaluating its ability to last and to understand the French market.

## Key takeaways

- A stable evaluation framework is more valuable than a list of actors that quickly becomes obsolete.
- Placing an actor in its category is the prerequisite for any comparison.
- Criteria: problem solved, user, model, compliance, integration, solidity.
- The durability of the actor matters as much as the features.
- Anchorage in the French framework is an advantage over international solutions.

# 22. How to implement a successful blended learning strategy

Blended learning combines face-to-face and remote delivery to draw on the strengths of each modality. A successful strategy does not juxtapose the two at random: it assigns to each modality what it does best, remote for content delivery and flexibility, face-to-face for practice, exchange and anchoring. Success rests on articulation, on the coherence of the overall pathway, and on the accompaniment that connects the sequences. A poorly articulated blended program cumulates the disadvantages of both modes rather than combining their strengths.

## The complementarity principle

Blended learning rests on a simple idea: each modality has its strengths. Asynchronous remote offers flexibility and autonomy for acquiring knowledge at one's own pace. Face-to-face or synchronous delivery brings interaction, supervised practice and group dynamics. Blended assigns to each what it does best, rather than doing everything in one default mode.

## Articulating sequences

The value of blended rests on articulation. The flipped classroom is the example: content delivery happens remotely in advance, face-to-face time is devoted to practice and exchange, of higher value. This articulation requires overall design: each sequence prepares or extends another. A pathway where remote and face-to-face coexist without connection loses the benefit of the combination.

## Accompanying and connecting

Blended requires accompaniment that connects sequences and sustains engagement between face-to-face moments. Tracking of remote phases, reminders, and a clear guiding thread prevent dropout in autonomous phases. Human accompaniment, trainer or tutor, ensures pathway continuity. The coherence perceived by the learner, who must understand how sequences connect, conditions success.

## Key takeaways

- Blended combines face-to-face and remote according to the strengths of each.
- Remote serves content delivery and flexibility; face-to-face serves practice and exchange.
- The flipped classroom illustrates the articulation: content remotely, practice face-to-face.
- Articulation requires overall design, not mere juxtaposition.
- Accompaniment connects sequences and sustains engagement between face-to-face times.

# 23. When is virtual reality relevant in training

Virtual reality is relevant in training when it delivers what no other modality can: immersion in a difficult, dangerous, costly or otherwise irreproducible situation. It excels for the practice of technical actions, simulation of risk situations, and learning through repeated practice without real-world consequences. Outside these use cases, its cost and complexity do not justify it against simpler modalities. VR is a high-impact niche tool for its specific use cases, not a universal modality.

## What VR specifically delivers

The unique value of virtual reality is immersion. It places the learner in a reconstructed situation where they act as in reality, with a physical and emotional engagement that other modalities do not reach. This immersion promotes anchoring of learning, particularly for actions and behaviors, and allows repetition without risk or material cost.

## Relevant use cases

VR is fully justified for practice in dangerous situations, safety, intervention in hazardous environments, where training in reality would expose the learner. It suits costly technical actions to reproduce, manipulation of expensive or rare equipment. It serves the simulation of difficult interpersonal situations, where repetition is possible without real consequences. In these cases, the pedagogical impact and the reduction in risk or cost justify the investment.

## Limitations to acknowledge

Outside its use cases, VR is rarely the right choice. Its production and equipment cost, deployment complexity and logistics make it disproportionate for transmitting knowledge that a standard module would cover. The temptation to adopt VR for its novelty effect leads to costly and poorly useful programs. The right approach starts from the need: if immersion adds nothing decisive, a simpler modality is preferable.

## Key takeaways

- VR delivers an immersion that no other modality achieves.
- It excels for dangerous situations, costly technical actions and interpersonal simulations.
- In these cases, impact and risk reduction justify the investment.
- Outside these cases, its cost and complexity are disproportionate.
- The approach starts from the need: without value from immersion, prefer simpler.

# 24. How to exploit my LMS data to improve training

Exploiting LMS data to improve training means transforming tracking information, enrolments, progression, results, dropout points, into concrete decisions to improve content and pathways. An LMS produces data by default, but few organizations genuinely exploit it. Value comes not from collection, which is automatic, but from analysis and action. Identifying where and why learners drop out, which content produces acquisition, makes it possible to adjust with method rather than intuition.

## Data available in an LMS

An LMS records by default a large amount of data: who enrolls, who progresses, who completes, who drops out, at which point, with what assessment results, in how much time. These data describe the actual functioning of training programs. The problem is not their availability but their exploitation: they often sit in dashboards consulted rarely and never translated into action.

## From data to analysis

Analysis looks for exploitable signals. A recurring dropout point flags content that is too difficult, too long or poorly placed. A high failure rate on an assessment questions alignment between content and test. Abnormally long time on a module reveals a difficulty. Crossing these signals with acquisition results connects activity to actually developed competencies, and directs improvement toward the real problems.

## From analysis to improvement

Data only has value when translated into action. Exploitation leads to decisions: rework a module with high dropout, shorten an overlong sequence, revise a poorly calibrated assessment, reinforce poorly acquired content. This continuous improvement approach, grounded in data rather than impressions, connects to the Qualiopi continuous improvement expectation for certified organizations. Personal data protection frames this exploitation.

## Key takeaways

- An LMS produces tracking data by default, rarely exploited.
- Value comes from analysis and action, not from collection.
- Dropout points, failure rates and time spent are exploitable signals.
- Crossing activity and acquisition directs improvement toward real problems.
- Exploitation connects to the continuous improvement expectation in Qualiopi.

# 25. How to migrate from one LMS to another without losing everything

Migrating from one LMS to another without data loss requires methodically preparing the transfer of content, learner data and history, drawing on technical standards to preserve portability. A poorly prepared migration loses content, training histories and user buy-in. Success rests on a prior audit of the existing setup, a phased migration plan, and a testing phase before cutover. Content portability, guaranteed by standards, is a central concern.

## Auditing the existing setup before migrating

A migration starts with an inventory: which content, in which formats, which learner data, what training history, what integrations with other systems. This audit reveals what is portable, what will need to be rebuilt, and the risks. Migrating without this inventory exposes you to discovering losses once the cutover is done, when it is too late to prevent them.

## Preserving portability through standards

Content compliant with standards such as SCORM or xAPI is in principle portable from one platform to another. Content created in an LMS's proprietary format may be difficult to export, creating lock-in. Migration is an opportunity to measure this lock-in and, going forward, to favor content in standard formats. Learner data and history require particular care, as their export format varies by platform.

## Migrating in phases and testing

A successful migration proceeds in phases rather than as a hard cutover. A plan defines the order of transfer, a testing phase verifies the integrity of content and data on the new platform, and a transition period limits risk. Accompanying users, trainers and learners, toward the new tool conditions buy-in: a technically successful migration that is poorly managed may fail through user rejection.

## Key takeaways

- Migration starts with an audit of the existing setup: content, data, integrations.
- Standards-compliant content is portable; proprietary formats create lock-in.
- Learner data and history require particular care in export.
- Phased migration with a testing phase limits risk.
- Accompanying users conditions buy-in for the new tool.

# 26. How to decide between building my own platform or buying one

Deciding between building your own platform and buying an existing solution rests on the classic build-versus-buy tradeoff: custom development offers full control and perfect fit to the need, but at high cost and risk; purchasing offers speed and controlled cost, at the price of adapting to the tool's constraints. For the large majority of organizations, purchasing a proven solution is preferable. Custom development is only justified by a very specific strategic need that no market solution covers.

## The true cost of custom development

Building your own platform does not stop at the initial construction cost. Ongoing maintenance, evolutions, security, regulatory compliance and support must be counted over the full lifetime of the tool. This total cost, often underestimated, is considerable. Custom development also mobilizes rare technical competencies and diverts the organization from its core business, which is rarely software publishing.

## The advantages of purchasing

Buying an existing solution brings speed to deployment, controlled entry cost, and the benefit of evolutions, security and compliance assured by the publisher. The counterpart is adaptation to the tool's constraints and dependence on the publisher. For most needs, these constraints are acceptable against the savings achieved. The market offers mature solutions covering the large majority of use cases.

## When custom development is justified

Custom development is only justified by a differentiating strategic need that no solution addresses, and when the organization has the capacity to carry a development and its maintenance over time. An intermediate path often exists: assembling existing building blocks, or relying on a production tool that covers the specific need without rebuilding everything. The decision is made by measuring total cost and risk, not just the appeal of custom.

## Key takeaways

- The tradeoff opposes full control of custom development and speed of purchasing.
- Custom development cost includes maintenance, evolutions, security and compliance.
- Custom development diverts from the core business and mobilizes rare competencies.
- Purchasing a proven solution suits the large majority of organizations.
- Custom development is only justified by a very specific strategic need.

# 27. How to integrate a third-party content catalogue into my training program

Integrating a third-party content catalogue, such as a course library from an external publisher, means complementing your offering with content produced by others, on generic or transversal subjects. The benefit is rapid access to a large volume of training programs without producing them. The limitation is the absence of customization and variable relevance to the specific organizational context. The right approach combines third-party catalogues for the generic and custom content for the specific and strategic, rather than delegating everything to a catalogue.

## The value of a third-party catalogue

A third-party content catalogue gives immediate access to a large volume of training on common topics: office software, languages, transversal competencies, awareness sessions. For these generic topics, producing in-house would make no sense. The third-party catalogue covers this need at a mutualized cost, with updating assured by the publisher. It usefully complements an offering on topics where specificity adds nothing.

## Limitations to be aware of

Third-party content is by definition generic. It does not know the organization's context, vocabulary, cases and priorities. On specific or strategic topics, this absence of customization costs in relevance and engagement: a learner quickly perceives content disconnected from their reality. The third-party catalogue therefore does not replace custom content where specificity matters.

## Combining third-party and custom

The right strategy combines both sources. Third-party catalogues cover the generic at lower cost; custom content handles the specific and strategic, where personalization creates value. This combination optimizes the cost-relevance ratio of the overall offering. The ability to produce custom content at a reasonable cost, through a generation engine, also broadens the share manageable as personalized, which was previously constrained by production cost.

## Key takeaways

- A third-party catalogue gives access to a large volume of generic content at mutualized cost.
- It covers transversal topics well where specificity adds nothing.
- Its limitation is the absence of customization to the organization's context.
- It does not replace custom content on specific and strategic topics.
- The right strategy combines third-party for the generic and custom for the specific.

# 28. How to integrate a third-party catalogue like LinkedIn Learning in a company

Integrating a third-party learning catalogue in a company, of the type offered by major online course publishers, requires defining its use, connecting it to the existing training program, and managing adoption so that it does not become an underused subscription. These catalogues provide broad access to content on transversal and digital competencies. Their main risk is low usage: a paid access that no one trains with. Value depends on accompanying usage, not on access alone.

## Defining the intended use

A third-party catalogue in a company can serve several purposes: open access for self-directed learning, recommended pathways on targeted competencies, complement to the skills development plan. Defining the intended use guides deployment. Open access without clear intent generally produces little effect; framed use, connected to development objectives, produces more. The subscription is only a means, not an end.

## Connecting to the existing program

The third-party catalogue connects to the rest of the program: skills development plan, custom training, internal pathways. It covers the generic and transversal, where in-house custom content handles the specific. Technically integrating the catalogue with the LMS or HR system, where possible, consolidates tracking and avoids siloed usage, disconnected from the overall training management.

## Steering adoption

The main pitfall of third-party catalogues in companies is under-utilization. Paid unlimited access that no one uses represents cost without return. Steering adoption requires communication, recommending pathways, manager involvement, and tracking actual usage. Usage data reveals whether the investment is producing effect and guides re-engagement actions. Without this steering, the catalogue becomes a recurring expense with uncertain value.

## Key takeaways

- A third-party catalogue in a company covers transversal and digital competencies.
- Defining the intended use guides deployment and conditions effect.
- It connects to the skills development plan and in-house custom content.
- Its main risk is under-utilization: paid access used by few.
- Steering adoption through communication, managers and usage tracking is essential.

# 29. When to use no-code tools for creating training

No-code tools allow training content and programs to be created without development technical expertise, through visual interfaces. They are relevant for empowering pedagogical teams, producing rapidly at lower cost, and prototyping. Their limitations appear for very specific needs, deep integration and very large-scale deployment. No-code is an autonomy accelerator, as long as ease of technical implementation is not confused with quality of pedagogical design, which remains the core of value.

## What no-code delivers

No-code removes the technical barrier. A pedagogical team can create modules, pathways and programs without depending on developers, through visual interfaces. This accelerates production, reduces costs and gives autonomy. No-code also enables rapid prototyping: testing a pedagogical idea before a larger investment. This autonomy is its main benefit.

## The limitations of no-code

No-code shows its limits on certain needs. Very specific features outside the tool's scope remain inaccessible. Deep integration with other systems may be constrained. Very large-scale deployment or particular performance requirements may exceed what the tool allows. No-code covers a wide range of common needs, but not the most demanding or singular cases.

## Not confusing technical with pedagogical

The trap of no-code is believing that technical ease resolves pedagogical difficulty. Creating a module without technical expertise does not dispense with designing quality learning: objectives, progression, aligned assessment. No-code accelerates formatting, not design. The value of training still rests on its pedagogical engineering. No-code is therefore a tool in service of design, not a substitute for pedagogical competency.

## Key takeaways

- No-code allows training to be created without development technical expertise.
- It empowers teams, accelerates production and facilitates prototyping.
- Its limitations: very specific needs, deep integration, very large scale.
- It covers common needs but not the most demanding or singular cases.
- It accelerates formatting without replacing pedagogical engineering.

# 30. How to prepare your organization for AI in training tools

Preparing an organization for AI in training tools requires anticipating three dimensions: upskilling training teams on these tools, revising production and management processes, and establishing governance covering quality, data and compliance. AI is not simply one more tool: it shifts value from production toward diagnosis and management. Organizations that prepare for it make it a lever; those that ignore it fall increasingly behind those that produce faster and better.

## Upskilling teams

The arrival of AI changes the competencies expected from training teams. The ability to diagnose needs, frame a generation, verify and manage becomes more determining than manual content production. Preparing the organization means training teams in these new competencies, reassuring them that AI shifts their value upward, toward design and management, rather than replacing them.

## Revising processes

AI changes the economics of production. What took weeks may take days; the bottleneck shifts from production toward the quality of needs diagnosis and the relevance of management. Processes must be revised accordingly: less time on manufacturing, more on needs analysis, verification and data exploitation. Maintaining processes designed for manual production deprives the organization of the gain AI brings.

## Installing governance

AI in training calls for governance. The quality of generated content must be controlled, as generation can produce errors. Learner personal data must be protected. Regulatory compliance, alignment with reference frameworks, traceability, accessibility requirements, must be maintained. Clear governance, defining who controls what and according to which criteria, makes it possible to benefit from AI without bearing its risks. This is the condition for controlled and durable use.

## Key takeaways

- AI shifts value from production toward diagnosis and management.
- Teams must develop competencies in framing, verification and management.
- Processes must be revised: less manufacturing, more analysis and control.
- Governance covers content quality, data protection and compliance.
- Preparing for it makes AI a lever; ignoring it creates a growing gap.
