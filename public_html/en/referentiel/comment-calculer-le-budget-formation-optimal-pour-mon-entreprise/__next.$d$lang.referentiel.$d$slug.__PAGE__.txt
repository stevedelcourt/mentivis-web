## Starting from need, not a ratio

Setting a training budget as a fixed percentage of payroll leads either to underinvestment or to spending without impact. The correct starting point is the need: which competencies to develop to achieve objectives. The budget flows from this prioritized need, not the other way around. Sector ratios serve as a coherence reference, not as a rule.

## Integrating financing into the calculation

The net cost of a training plan is often far below its gross cost, thanks to mobilizable financing: OPCO coverage, employer-topped CPF, specific mechanisms. Calculating the budget without integrating these levers leads to overestimating the cost and underinvesting. Financing optimization increases the volume of achievable actions at a constant direct effort, which changes the budget equation.

## Arbitrating between volume and quality

The same budget can finance many superficial training sessions or a few high-impact ones. The arbitration between volume and quality depends on the priorities: some critical competencies justify deep investment, others can be covered by lighter formats. Average cost per trained employee is a management indicator, to be interpreted against the impact sought, not as an end in itself.

## Key takeaways

- The optimal budget starts from the prioritized need, not an arbitrary ratio.
- Sector benchmarks serve as a reference, not as a rule.
- The net cost integrates financing and is often far below the gross cost.
- Financing optimization increases volume at a constant direct effort.
