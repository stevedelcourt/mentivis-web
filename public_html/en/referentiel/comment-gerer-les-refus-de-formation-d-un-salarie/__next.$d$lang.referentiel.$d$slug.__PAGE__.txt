## Distinguishing mandatory from optional training

Training made mandatory by regulation or necessary for carrying out the job is binding on the employee, and unjustified refusal may constitute a disciplinary matter. Conversely, training falling under skills development without a mandatory character cannot in principle be imposed against the employee's will, especially if it takes place outside working hours. This distinction defines the employer's margin.

## Understanding the reason for the refusal

A refusal almost always has a cause: a personal scheduling constraint, apprehension about the content, a sense of irrelevance, a poor past experience, or disagreement on relevance. Dialogue identifies this cause. Often, addressing the obstacle, adjusting the schedule, reassuring, explaining the benefit, resolves the refusal more effectively than an instruction. The professional interview is a suitable framework for this exchange.

## Connecting to the employer's obligations

The employee's refusal does not extinguish the employer's obligations, in particular the adaptation obligation and the potential triggering of the corrective top-up in cases of failure. The employer therefore has an interest in documenting training offers made and any refusals received, which demonstrates compliance with obligations. This traceability protects the company while keeping the dialogue open.

## Key takeaways

- Refusal of mandatory training may be a disciplinary matter; refusal of optional training is in principle legitimate.
- Optional training outside working hours cannot be imposed.
- Understanding the cause of the refusal often resolves it through dialogue.
- The professional interview is a suitable framework for this exchange.
