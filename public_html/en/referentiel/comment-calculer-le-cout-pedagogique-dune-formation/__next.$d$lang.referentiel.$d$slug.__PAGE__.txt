## Distinguishing direct and indirect costs

Direct costs are traceable to a specific action: trainer fees or loaded salary, materials design, room rental, platform licenses, travel expenses. Indirect costs cover general operations: administration, sales, pedagogical oversight, permanent premises. An allocation key, often based on hour volume, distributes a share of indirect costs to each action.

## From cost price to pricing

The total cost price divided by hour volume gives the hourly cost. Divided by the number of learners, it gives the per-learner cost, a key indicator for group training where pooling reduces the unit cost.

Pricing adds an operational margin to the cost price. It is also assessed against the market and OPCO coverage levels, which effectively cap the acceptable price for certain actions.

## Managing profitability by module

Tracking cost by module makes it possible to identify profitable actions and those that weigh on the organization. A module with low enrolment may be loss-making despite a high listed price, if direct costs are not covered. Management consists in arbitrating between volume, price and pooling, and deciding whether to maintain, adjust or discontinue an action.

## Key takeaways

- Pedagogical cost adds direct costs and a share of indirect costs.
- Hourly cost and per-learner cost are the two key indicators.
- Pricing adds a margin to the cost price, within the limits of the market and coverage levels.
- Pooling reduces the unit cost of group training.
